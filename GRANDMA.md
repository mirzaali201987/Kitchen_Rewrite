# GRANDMA.md

A plain-language story about the app I built, written for someone who has never touched a computer for anything other than looking at photos of the grandchildren.

---

## Let me tell you what I made

Suppose you have a recipe for chocolate chip cookies that your mother made, and they were your favourite thing in the world. Now suppose the doctor says you can't have butter anymore. Or one of your grandchildren comes over and can't have peanuts. Or your daughter-in-law is trying to eat less sugar.

You don't want to give up the cookies. You want the same cookies, just made in a way that works for whoever is going to eat them.

What I built is a helper for exactly that. You tell it the recipe. You tell it what you can't eat. It hands you back two new versions of the same recipe that both work, with a note explaining what it changed and why.

That's the whole thing.

---

## Think of it like a little shop

Imagine a small shop on a quiet street. It has a front door and a back door. Inside, there are a few rooms. Each room has one person in it, and each person does one thing. That's really all this is.

Let me walk you through what happens when you come to visit.

---

## You walk in the front door

At the front of the shop there is a counter. On the counter there is a clipboard and a jar of little paper tags.

The clipboard is for writing down the recipe. You write out the cookies exactly the way your mother made them. Flour, butter, sugar, eggs, the lot.

The tags in the jar each have a word on them. "No dairy." "No nuts." "No meat." "Less sugar." You pick up the tags that apply to whoever you're cooking for and lay them next to your clipboard.

When you're ready, there's a little brass bell on the counter. You ring it.

---

## The runner picks up your papers

A young runner comes out from the back, picks up your clipboard and your tags, and hurries down the hallway to the kitchen. He does this in about the time it takes you to blink. You don't see any of it.

You stand at the counter. You wait. Something quiet is happening behind the scenes.

---

## The kitchen and the cook

At the end of the hallway there is a small kitchen. In the kitchen there is a cook. The cook takes the clipboard, reads the recipe, and then looks at the tags you chose.

The first thing the cook does is check the recipe is reasonable. Is there anything actually written on the paper? Is it not ten thousand words long? Did you pick at least one tag? If something seems wrong, the cook politely sends your papers back with a note explaining the problem, and you see a message on the counter saying "sorry, try again."

If everything looks fine, the cook walks across the kitchen to the next room.

---

## The librarian and her book

Next to the kitchen there is a small office. In the office there is a librarian and one book. That's it. One book.

The book is thin. I wrote it myself, by hand, over several evenings. Every page is a tested ingredient swap. For example, one page says:

> "If a recipe calls for butter, and the person can't have dairy, use three-quarters of a cup of olive oil or coconut oil instead of one cup of butter. Coconut oil keeps baked goods closer to the original. Olive oil is better for savoury cooking."

Another page says:

> "If a recipe calls for pine nuts, and the person can't have nuts, use pumpkin seeds or hemp seeds instead. Same amount. Toast them lightly first."

There are about twenty of these pages. That's the whole book.

The cook says to the librarian, "I need the pages about no-dairy and no-nuts." The librarian flips through the book, finds those pages, and hands them across. Pages about "less sugar" and "no meat" stay in the book for another day.

This is important. The cook is only allowed to use swaps that are in this book. She is not allowed to invent new ones. I will explain why in a moment.

---

## The cook writes a careful letter

Now the cook has two things on her counter: your recipe, and the pages from the book.

She takes out a piece of paper and writes a very careful letter. The letter is addressed to a kitchen assistant. It says, roughly:

> "Dear assistant. I have this recipe, and this person cannot eat these things. I am attaching some ingredient swaps. You must only use these swaps. Do not invent any of your own. Rewrite the recipe in two different ways, so the person has a choice. For each change you make, tell me what you swapped and why. If something cannot be solved with the swaps I gave you, just say so — do not pretend to fix it. If the person has an allergy, remind them to check the labels on the packages they buy, because even flour can sometimes have traces of other things."

The cook puts this letter, the recipe, and the pages into an envelope.

---

## The assistant in the distant kitchen

Now, the cook doesn't do the actual rewriting herself. She sends the envelope far away, to a much bigger kitchen that belongs to someone else. In that kitchen there is a very well-read apprentice. Think of someone who has spent years reading every cookbook in existence and has a very good feel for food. That apprentice doesn't know you, doesn't know your cookies, doesn't know what you want — they only know what the letter tells them.

The apprentice reads our cook's letter carefully. They follow the instructions. They write back with two rewritten versions of your recipe, notes on what they changed, and warnings if they need to add any.

A few seconds later, the envelope comes back through the post. Our cook opens it.

**Why send it far away?** Because this apprentice is very clever, and it would take me many lifetimes to train one of my own. The people in that distant kitchen let us send them a small number of letters each hour for free, which is plenty for our little shop.

---

## The cook double-checks the reply

Before handing the reply to the runner, our cook reads it carefully.

There is one thing she always checks: if one of your tags was about an allergy, did the apprentice include a warning about checking labels? If the apprentice forgot, our cook adds the warning herself. She doesn't trust just one person for something as serious as an allergy. She adds a second check of her own.

This is the kind of thing that matters. A clever apprentice is still just one person, and even a clever person can forget.

---

## The runner also fetches a photograph

While the cook has been working, the runner has been busy on a second errand.

Two doors down from the kitchen there is a photographer's shop. The runner pops in with a short note: "We just rewrote a recipe for chocolate chip cookies. Do you have a photograph we can show?"

The photographer has a relationship with a very large photo library across town. She looks up "chocolate chip cookies" in their catalogue, picks a nice picture, and hands it to the runner. She also hands over a small card with the name of whoever took the photograph, because the library asks us to always credit the person who took it. That's a fair rule and we follow it.

The runner tucks the photograph under his arm and heads back to the front of the shop.

**Why does the photographer have the relationship with the library, and not you?** Because the library gave us a secret password, and we don't want to hand that password out to every visitor who walks through our door. We keep the password in the back office. Only the photographer knows it. Visitors never see it.

---

## You get your result

The runner arrives back at the counter. He lays out for you:

- The photograph of the cookies, with a small credit line underneath
- A warm little note if any of your tags were an allergy, reminding you to check packaging
- Two cards side by side, each with a rewritten version of the recipe
- For each card: the list of ingredients, the numbered steps, and a small panel showing exactly what changed and why — which swap came from which page of the book

You can read either version. You can pick the one that sounds better to you. You can write it down, or print it, or cook from it tonight.

That's the whole visit. You came in with a recipe you couldn't use. You leave with two recipes you can.

---

## Some things about this shop that I want you to know

### The cook is not allowed to make things up

This is the most important thing. The cook follows the book, and only the book. If something isn't in the book — say you try to cook with saffron, and there's no page about saffron — she leaves it alone and tells you honestly.

Why does this matter? Because a clever apprentice who makes things up can sound convincing while being wrong. If the apprentice told you "peanut butter is fine for your grandchild with a nut allergy," someone could end up in hospital. By forcing the cook to only use tested swaps from a book I wrote by hand, we keep the whole thing honest.

### The shop says no to a lot of things

I could have added many more rooms. A room where you upload a photo of a recipe from a cookbook and the shop reads it for you. A room that finds restaurants near you that make the dish. A room that remembers every recipe you've ever adapted. I thought about all of them.

I decided not to build any of them. Each would have taken days and left the main thing half-finished. A small shop that works well is more useful than a big shop where half the rooms are locked.

### There are things the shop can't do, and I'm honest about them

It can't guarantee a recipe is safe for someone with a serious allergy. It can remind you to check labels, but it can't see inside a packet of flour to know whether the packet was filled in a factory that also handled peanuts. That is still your job.

It can't read a picture of a recipe. You have to type out the words.

It is not a doctor. It is not a dietitian. It is a kitchen helper.

---

## What I actually learned from building this

Three things.

**The hardest word to say is no.** When you're making something, everyone has ideas — including yourself. Every new idea sounds good in the moment. Learning to say "not this time, maybe later" is most of what makes a project actually finish.

**Clever isn't the same as right.** The apprentice in the distant kitchen is very clever. She can also confidently tell you something wrong. The work of giving her a book she has to stick to, instead of letting her guess, is what turns a magic trick into a real tool.

**Simple is better than big.** I kept waiting for the moment when I'd need to add more rooms to the shop. That moment never came. What was there was enough.

## If you want to use it

Go to the website. Paste in a recipe — one of your own, or one from a cookbook, or one you saw in the paper. Pick the tags that apply to whoever is going to eat the dish. Ring the bell.

In a few seconds, you'll have two new versions of the recipe you brought in.

If that helps you cook for someone you couldn't cook for before, then the shop has done what it was built to do.
